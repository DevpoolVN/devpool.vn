# Writing Standard

The in-house style for blog posts in this template. Distilled from how the publications and writers below actually work.

**Influences worth knowing about:**

- **The New York Times ("The Daily", magazine longform)** — restrained voice, specific named details, sentence rhythm that varies on purpose.
- **The Atlantic / The New Yorker** — argument-driven essays where every claim earns its place.
- **National Geographic** — sense of place is non-negotiable; cultural context is woven into scenes, not lectured.
- **Lonely Planet / Atlas Obscura** — friendly, plainspoken, scannable; honest tips; quirky angles that earn the "huh, didn't know that" payoff.
- **Bourdain (Parts Unknown, Kitchen Confidential)** — subject as a window into people; local voices centered; humility about being a guest.
- **Patrick McKenzie, Julia Evans, Dan Luu** (technical bloggers) — precise specifics; real numbers; honest about uncertainty.
- **Independent voices worth borrowing from** — Nomadic Matt (practical specificity), Legal Nomads (long-form research depth), Maggie Appleton (visual essays with editorial discipline).

What unites the good ones isn't a style — it's a _discipline_. Specifics over generics. Scenes over summaries. People over places. Honesty over hype.

---

## 1. Voice — five sliders

Treat the voice as five sliders, not a single setting. Different posts pull them to different positions.

| Slider                    | Low end                                            | High end                                                  |
| ------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Informative ↔ Lyrical** | A how-to guide.                                    | A meditation on a topic.                                  |
| **Useful ↔ Reflective**   | "Bring small bills."                               | "The smell of lemongrass at dawn is what brings me back." |
| **Wry ↔ Earnest**         | A self-aware aside about an obvious failure mode.  | Straightforward respect for a hard-won craft.             |
| **Insider ↔ Newcomer**    | "The folks who actually use it call it Y, not X."  | "On my first day, I got every keyboard shortcut wrong."   |
| **Confident ↔ Humble**    | "Here's why most teams get this wrong."            | "I'm still figuring out where this breaks."               |

Defaults: lean toward Informative-leaning, Useful, gently Wry, Insider-with-humility, and Confident-but-honest-about-limits.

## 2. Structural movement

Every post has three movements: **hook → development → close**. Structure is _what the reader feels at each beat_, not just headings.

### The hook (~1–2 short paragraphs)

The first paragraph is a promise. It says: this post is going to be specific and worth your time.

**Patterns that work:**

- **In medias res.** Drop the reader into a moment. "The lanterns went out one street at a time, beginning at the river."
- **A specific person.** "Bà Tám has sold sticky rice from the same corner for forty-one years."
- **A small contradiction.** "Hội An calls itself a 'living museum.' For about three hours each evening, it is genuinely both."
- **A useful, specific fact.** "The 14th day of every lunar month is the only night Hội An turns off the streetlights."
- **A claim, stated plainly.** "Most teams reach for Redux about two years too early."

**Patterns that don't work:**

- "Imagine yourself walking through…" — Generic prompt.
- "Welcome to [topic]!" — Sounds like a brochure.
- "Have you ever wondered…?" — The reader hasn't.
- "Located in central Vietnam, X is a UNESCO site…" — Wikipedia summary.
- "In today's fast-paced world…" — Hard delete.

### The development (the bulk of the post)

This is where structure-as-headings comes in. Pick a structural pattern from `content-format.md` that fits the topic. Within each section:

- Each section should have its own micro-arc — a small hook, a small payoff.
- Alternate texture: prose, then a list, then a callout, then back to prose.
- Quote real people when you can. A 12-word quote is worth a paragraph of summary.

### The close (~1–2 short paragraphs)

Don't recap. Instead:

- **Send the reader somewhere.** A next read, a specific date, an action.
- **Return to the opening image.** Echo a phrase or scene from the hook.
- **Make a small, honest claim.** "It is not the most famous festival in Vietnam. It is the one most worth your evening."

A late-stage brand/project mention, if used, lives here. One sentence. No exclamation marks.

## 3. Sentence-level discipline

### Specific over generic

| Don't                  | Do                                                                                                                                                            |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "a local vendor"       | "Bà Tám, who has run the corner stall since 1983"                                                                                                             |
| "delicious local food" | "cao lầu — square noodles with pork, herbs, and a topping of crisp rice crackers — served only in Hội An because the recipe needs water from a specific well" |
| "stunning views"       | "the Thu Bồn River widens here, and at dusk you can see all the way to Cẩm Kim Island"                                                                        |
| "very cheap"           | "around 30,000₫ — roughly $1.20 — for a full bowl"                                                                                                            |
| "very fast"            | "the new build dropped from 24 seconds to 6"                                                                                                                  |
| "ancient"              | "first built in 1593, rebuilt twice, last in 1763"                                                                                                            |

If you reach for "stunning", "vibrant", "beautiful", "magical", "breathtaking", "blazing-fast", "next-level", or "game-changing", stop and replace it with the thing that _would_ make a reader feel that way.

### Sensory anchoring (when applicable)

For travel, food, or place writing: use all five senses, not just sight. For technical writing: use precise units, numbers, and named tools.

### Show, don't tell

| Tell                                    | Show                                                                                                                                                                                   |
| --------------------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "Hội An is a beautiful, peaceful town." | "On Phan Châu Trinh, three teenagers play badminton in the middle of the street. No one honks at them. There is no one to honk."                                                       |
| "The build is fast."                    | "Edit a single component, save, see the change in the browser before your thumb leaves the keyboard."                                                                                  |
| "The food is fresh."                    | "The herbs come from a garden you can see from the kitchen window."                                                                                                                    |

### Sentence rhythm

Vary sentence length deliberately. Three short sentences in a row creates urgency. A single long sentence with three clauses creates drift. Use both.

### Word choice — the watch list

These words and phrases are _suspect_. Each is a flag you may be coasting on cliché.

- **"Nestled"** — almost never the right word; replace with what the place is next to.
- **"Vibrant"** — meaningless. What color? What sound?
- **"In the heart of"** — overused.
- **"Boasts"** — places and tools don't brag.
- **"A must-visit / must-read / must-try"** — service-journalism cliché.
- **"Hidden gem"** — usually neither; if it is, prove it.
- **"Mecca for [X]"** — culturally insensitive; replace.
- **"Vibrant tapestry / rich tapestry"** — instant AI-flavor. Delete.
- **"Embark on a journey"** — never.
- **"Exotic"** — to whom? Cut.
- **"Authentic"** — usually a hedge for "I don't know how to describe what makes this real". Be specific.
- **"Mouth-watering"** — describe the taste.
- **"Bustling"** — describe the bustle.
- **"Delve into"** — say "look at" or "examine".
- **"In today's fast-paced world…"** — instant delete.
- **"Game-changer / paradigm shift / revolutionary"** — almost always overstatement.
- **"Robust / scalable / cutting-edge / next-generation"** — give the number or the property.
- **"Whether you're a beginner or an expert…"** — the everyone-listicle hedge.

### Cultural and technical care

- **Use proper names first.** "Phố Cổ (the Old Town)", "Server-Sent Events (SSE)".
- **Get spelling right.** "Hội An" (not "Hoi An") in the body; "TypeScript" (not "Typescript").
- **Don't speak for groups.** "Locals love…" / "Developers think…" — red flag. Quote someone specific.
- **Be honest about trade-offs.** Acknowledging downsides builds trust.
- **Avoid the "discover" frame.** The place wasn't lost. The technique wasn't invented yesterday.

## 4. Anti-patterns specific to AI drafts

- Three-adjective stacking: "this charming, vibrant, ancient town" / "this fast, scalable, robust framework"
- The grand abstract close: "X is more than a Y — it is a feeling, a memory, a piece of yourself you didn't know you'd left behind." Delete.
- Symmetric paragraphs that all start with "When you…" or "As you…"
- "Whether you're a foodie, a culture lover, or a budget traveler…" — the everyone-listicle hedge.
- Section headings that are pure adjectives: "Stunning Scenery", "Powerful Features". Replace with concrete nouns.
- Numbered lists where the numbers don't matter.
- Closing with a question to the reader.
- The em-dash overload — when every other sentence has — an aside — like — this. Trim.

## 5. Worked examples

### Example A — opening paragraph

**Draft (weak):**

> Welcome to Hội An, a charming, ancient town in central Vietnam known for its vibrant lantern festival. Every month, locals and tourists gather to celebrate this stunning cultural tradition. In this post, we'll explore everything you need to know about the Hội An Lantern Festival.

**Rewrite:**

> The streetlights on Trần Phú go out one block at a time, beginning at the river. By the time the dark reaches the Japanese Bridge, the lanterns are already lit — a few thousand of them, paper and silk in red, orange, and a yellow so warm it looks edible. This happens on the 14th day of every lunar month. The town has been doing it, in some form, for four hundred years.

### Example B — practical info

**Draft (weak):**

> The lantern festival is a must-visit event that takes place monthly. Visitors should arrive early to avoid the crowds and bring their cameras to capture the beautiful moments.

**Rewrite:**

> Arrive by 6 p.m. if you want to walk the streets before the river fills with boats. By 8, Trần Phú is shoulder-to-shoulder and the river boats are sold out for the night. Cash only at most of the stalls; the closest ATM that takes foreign cards is in front of the Vinpearl on Cửa Đại Road, about ten minutes away on foot.

### Example C — closing line

**Draft (weak):**

> The Hội An Lantern Festival is truly a magical experience that you'll never forget. Whether you're a culture lover, a photographer, or just someone seeking adventure, this is an event you don't want to miss!

**Rewrite:**

> The streetlights come back on around eleven. The lanterns stay lit a little longer — until the families who hung them come out to take them down, one block at a time, beginning at the river.

### Example D — technical opening

**Draft (weak):**

> In today's fast-paced web development landscape, choosing the right framework is more important than ever. Astro is a game-changing tool that empowers developers to build blazing-fast websites.

**Rewrite:**

> Astro ships ~5 KB of JavaScript to the browser by default, and only adds more if you specifically ask for an island. After a year of using it, that constraint has changed how I write components — not because I had to, but because I noticed I never wanted to.

## 6. Post-type tonal presets

- **Festival / event** — Lyrical-leaning, Reflective, Earnest. Lead with scene; close with scene.
- **Deep-dive (a craft, a tradition, a technology)** — Informative, Useful, Earnest, Insider-with-humility.
- **Food/drink feature** — Informative-with-sensory, Useful, gently Wry, Insider.
- **Place portrait** — Lyrical, Reflective, Earnest.
- **How-to / guide** — Informative, Useful, gently Wry, Insider.
- **Human story** — Lyrical-leaning, Reflective, Earnest. The person is the protagonist.
- **Listicle-with-narrative** — Informative, Useful, gently Wry.
- **Opinion / argument** — Confident, Earnest about the parts that are uncertain. Concede something real.

## 7. The final editing pass

Before saving the file, re-read with these questions:

1. **Could a reader name three specific things they didn't know before?** If no, add specifics.
2. **Is there a real person quoted, named, or scene-set anywhere?** If no, add one (where appropriate).
3. **Does the first paragraph promise something concrete?** If it could open any article on this topic, rewrite.
4. **Are there any words from the watchlist?** Cut or replace.
5. **Does the close return to the opening or send the reader somewhere?** If it just rephrases the intro, rewrite.
6. **For opinion pieces — did I concede anything?**
7. **Proper names spelled right?**

A draft that survives this pass is publishable.
